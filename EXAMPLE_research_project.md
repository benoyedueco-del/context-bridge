### SECTION 1: PROJECT KERNEL

This is a comparative research project analyzing carbon pricing mechanisms across 12 jurisdictions to identify design features that correlate with emissions reductions without causing economic disruption. It serves a policy research institute producing a flagship report for government and NGO audiences. The project's core value is the analytical framework that scores each jurisdiction's carbon pricing design across 8 dimensions — without this framework, the comparison is just a collection of case studies, not actionable policy guidance.

### SECTION 7: WHAT FAILED OR SURPRISED

[2026-01-15] [FAILURE] [RECALLED] — First attempt at the scoring framework had 11 dimensions. Pilot testing against 3 jurisdictions revealed that 3 dimensions were unmeasurable with publicly available data. Framework was reduced to 8 dimensions. Lesson: validate data availability before finalizing an analytical framework.

[2026-02-05] [SURPRISE] [RECALLED] — British Columbia carbon tax data showed a pattern inconsistent with the literature's predictions. Initial assumption was a data error. Further research confirmed it was a genuine anomaly worth featuring in the analysis, not discarding.

### SECTION 10: COLD-START BRIEFING FOR RECEIVING AI

If you are a new AI picking up this work, here is what you need to know:

1. This is a comparative policy research project, not a position paper. The goal is analytical rigor, not advocacy.
2. The 8-dimension scoring framework is the entire analytical contribution of the report. Understand it before touching anything.
3. Active work: completing data collection for Canada and South Korea, then writing the comparative analysis chapter.
4. Do not write policy recommendations until the comparative analysis chapter is complete. This is a hard rule.
5. The British Columbia anomaly is real and interesting — do not treat it as a data error.
6. The human is a policy researcher, highly analytical, prefers structured output, and reacts poorly to advocacy framing.
7. Canada data has pre-2019 gaps. Do not assume continuity — ask how to handle them.
8. South Korea data requires Korean-language source assistance. Flag this before attempting data collection.
9. Dimension weighting for the overall score is an open problem. Do not invent a weighting scheme without discussion.
10. The immediate next action is: resolve the Canada data gap methodology, then complete Canada data collection.

### SECTION 13: GLOSSARY

ETS: Emissions Trading System — a cap-and-trade mechanism that sets a total emissions cap and allows participants to buy and sell allowances.
CARBON TAX: A direct price on carbon emissions, typically set per tonne of CO2 equivalent.
8-DIMENSION FRAMEWORK: The analytical tool that scores each jurisdiction's carbon pricing design on: price level, coverage breadth, price stability, revenue use, border adjustment, phase-in design, sectoral exemptions, and enforcement mechanisms.
RGGI: Regional Greenhouse Gas Initiative — a cap-and-trade program covering 11 northeastern US states.
ECONOMIC DISRUPTION METRIC: As defined in this project — sectoral employment impact and energy price index change in the 3 years post-implementation.
